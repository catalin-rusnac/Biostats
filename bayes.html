Bayes' Theorem

Introduction to Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' Theorem is a fundamental theorem in probability theory that describes how to update the probabilities of hypotheses when given evidence.

Formula:

\(P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \times P(A)}{P(B)}\)

Where:

  • \(P(A|B)\) is the posterior probability: the probability of hypothesis A given the evidence B
  • \(P(B|A)\) is the likelihood: the probability of evidence B given that hypothesis A is true
  • \(P(A)\) is the prior probability: the initial probability of hypothesis A
  • \(P(B)\) is the evidence: the total probability of the evidence B

Detailed Calculations

Positive Predictive Value (PPV): The probability that a person has the disease given a positive test result.

\(PPV = P(Disease|Positive) = \frac{P(Positive|Disease) \times P(Disease)}{P(Positive)}\)

Where:

  • \(P(Positive|Disease)\) = Sensitivity = True Positive Rate = 0.95
  • \(P(Disease)\) = Prevalence = 0.01
  • \(P(Positive)\) = \(P(Positive|Disease) \times P(Disease) + P(Positive|No Disease) \times P(No Disease)\)
  • \(P(Positive|No Disease)\) = False Positive Rate = 0.05
  • \(P(No Disease)\) = 1 - Prevalence = 0.99

Plugging in the numbers:

\(PPV = \frac{0.95 \times 0.01}{0.95 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99} = \frac{0.0095}{0.059} = 0.161\)

Negative Predictive Value (NPV): The probability that a person does not have the disease given a negative test result.

\(NPV = P(No Disease|Negative) = \frac{P(Negative|No Disease) \times P(No Disease)}{P(Negative)}\)

Where:

  • \(P(Negative|No Disease)\) = Specificity = True Negative Rate = 0.95
  • \(P(No Disease)\) = 1 - Prevalence = 0.99
  • \(P(Negative)\) = \(P(Negative|Disease) \times P(Disease) + P(Negative|No Disease) \times P(No Disease)\)
  • \(P(Negative|Disease)\) = False Negative Rate = 0.05
  • \(P(Disease)\) = Prevalence = 0.01

Plugging in the numbers:

\(NPV = \frac{0.95 \times 0.99}{0.05 \times 0.01 + 0.95 \times 0.99} = \frac{0.9405}{0.941} = 0.999\)

Bayesian Inference

Bayesian inference is a method of statistical inference in which Bayes' theorem is used to update the probability for a hypothesis as more evidence or information becomes available.

Process:

  1. Start with a prior probability \(P(A)\)
  2. Collect evidence \(B\)
  3. Calculate the likelihood \(P(B|A)\)
  4. Apply Bayes' Theorem to get the posterior probability \(P(A|B)\)
  5. Use the posterior as the new prior for the next update

As we collect more evidence, our belief about a hypothesis becomes more certain. If the evidence supports the hypothesis, our belief becomes stronger; if the evidence contradicts the hypothesis, our belief becomes weaker. This is the essence of Bayesian updating.

Question 10: In sequential Bayesian updates, what happens to the prior probability in each step?

In sequential Bayesian updates, the posterior probability from one step becomes the prior probability for the next step. This allows us to incorporate new evidence while building upon our previous knowledge.
